Birth of a Data Strategy

Chapter One: Website Analytics

Introduction

The Data Strategy was the largest project undertaken by the Data Hub. We set out to make something unlike a document, ambitious in its design and content. We learned a lot.

Yet as websites go, the data strategy was straightforward. The audience was small, the purpose was simple. So this report is a bit of an experiment. The goal is threefold:

  • 1. To see how much knowledge we can possibly learn from a small web project.
  • 2. To narrow in on the user experience, qualitatively.
  • 3. To provide (and in some cases demonstrate) recommendations for future digital products.

Chapter One will look at macro-level web analytics, providing an overview of website performance and engagement. These data are pulled from Google Analytics, which is widely used to understand web traffic for marketing purposes.

Chapter Two is going to look qualitatively at the user experience. This chapter uses Hotjar as a primary source of data. Hotjar provides a visual representation of how users interact with the website, showing mouse movements, clicks, and so on.

Chapter Three will be an incursion of the mind: an exploration of consciousness, challenging the very fabric of realit-- just kidding there is no chapter three.

Traffic

We started collecting data on September 2nd. Since then we've seen 596 new users, representing roughly 22% of the total staff at PHAC. We don't know for sure how many of those users were 'truly' unique. There could be duplication from users who used different devices, but also a lack of data from browsers that block tracking scripts. This is a rough count of a small sample size, so put away your graphing calculators: we aren't going to be doing any regression analyses here.

For now let's look at the patterns and spikes in traffic.

In the chart above you can see that traffic spiked on several occasions. There are a few observations we can draw from this traffic data:

1. Traffic did not grow steadily

This is because only 26% of visitors returned, which just means that people aren't coming back to read the data strategy every day. This may not be a problem: perhaps an introduction to the data strategy is enough to inspire confidence and innovation throughout the organization. I do, however, think that we had the opportunity to create some reference-style materials that might encourage users to bookmark the page and return. I will get to that in the recommendations at the end of this chapter.

2. Traffic spiked on days when we ran announcements in the Broadcast News thingy

This could tell us something about the effectiveness of Broadcast News, but we don't have anything to compare it to. As you will see in the next section, there were limited means by which we could draw traffic to the GCpedia page.

3. Data strategies are for 9-5

If you look closely, you can see that our poor lonely website was alone almost every weekend and holiday. Maybe that's because people don't want to read data strategies on their time off, but even if they did this would require booting up the 'ol PHAC-issued Fujitsu, signing into the VPN, finding the link in the Broadcast News email, and waiting the full 12 seconds it takes for GCpedia to load the page. I'm going to talk about GCpedia more below, but we can already see how these barriers tend to filter out casual web traffic.

Audience

At first glance Google Analytics doesn't say much about our audience. We already know that our audience was restricted to those who have access to GCpedia. This is unique because it means that users weren't being brought in by search engines or external links. Internal websites discourage sharing on public forums like social media, so these organic methods of attracting an audience were reduced. Furthermore, as a platform, GCpedia doesn't generate traffic. Instead, users were brought in by the aforementioned Broadcast News announcements, as well as our kickass guerrilla marketing campaign.

So we don't have anything interesting to learn about how we acquired an audience. What we can see, however, is the peculiar way in which users access internal government websites. Specifically, we see a disproportionate number of our visitors using Internet Explorer.

This is interesting because IE is an outdated and risky browser. Globally most users (all but 5%) have moved to other browsers, while our website saw 51% using IE.

I know this isn't blowing your mind. Although Microsoft replaced and ended support for IE in 2015, most PHAC-issued computers use IE by default. Moreover, even though many PHAC employees have moved over to Chrome, I suspect that the average PHAC employee assumes that IE should be used for all internal websites. After all, IE is the only browser that will open many internal web-based applications (lookin' at you, Phoenix).

Still, the disproportionate number of people using this outdated browser puts a number on how out of date we are. The last time IE held above 50% of the global share of web browsers was 2010. I wonder if by creating internal websites we are contributing to the sustained use of outdated and insecure technology. At the very least it falls well short of our duty to be innovative.

This tells us that we didn't make a particularly interactive website, but it doesn't necessarily mean that people never actually engaged with the text.

That's because the 'session' metric is flawed. Or rather, it's tuned for a different purpose (marketing). It's useful if you're trying to convert traffic into clicks or sales, but it doesn't look at qualitative interactions with the page. Google Analytics cannot track how much the user is reading, nor any interaction with elements that don't involve loading new information. This is why it's important to use a tool like Hotjar to look qualitatively at user engagement, which we will get into in the next chapter.

For now, we can get a clue as to why the average session duration is so low by looking at the distribution.

In the table above you can see that session durations weren't evenly distributed. The average was frontloaded, with 65% of sessions lasting less than 10 seconds. It's also interesting to note that Chrome users averaged significantly longer session durations (3:14) compared to IE users (1:34).

When you consider that almost 30% of users returned for multiple sessions, 3 groups of users emerge:

  1. Users who were instantly engaged.
  2. Users who returned to engage with the content later.
  3. Users who arrived at the page but never returned.

If we want to increase engagement numbers then we're going to have to look deeper at these 3 groups of users. Stay tuned for chapter two!

Conclusion

Looking forward, many key aspects of our mandate involve delivering innovative digital services, projects, and general web communications. This chapter gave us a look at some of the quantitative metrics that will help guide those future projects. We also saw some hints at the user experience lessons that we will uncover in the next chapter.

Recommendations

Community Building

We learned that only 26% of users returned for a second visit. This may not be a concern, after all most people don't need to look at a data strategy every day.

Then again, there are always opportunities to make useful things that encourage return visits. If we want to improve user retention, we need to give them something they'll want to bookmark and use later. The UK Policy Lab, for example, includes an "Open Policy Making Toolkit" on their website. Because they are providing something useful, I have returned to the website many times, and here I am linking to it in this report, expanding their audience.

This is about asking the right 'user research questions'. The data strategy website served a purpose, but did it add value to its users? Did it address a user need? Maybe somewhere in the midst of our long-term planning and culture-shifting we need to check to make sure we are also making practical things for our users.

Going forward we have a lot of opportunities to provide resources for PHAC employees, from data inventories to data science toolkits. We can use these resources to help build an audience and ultimately a stronger and more innovative public health community. Here's what I recommend:

  • Conduct user testing earlier in the design process

    This will help inform design decisions based on user feedback.

  • Work and prototype in the open

    Working in public will build an audience organically and keep people engaged (maybe even on weekends).

  • Ask good user research questions

    This will help us understand what people really need, and how to build practical tools that keep them engaged.

Inspiring confidence

In our engagement section we identified three different groups of users:

  1. Those who were instantly engaged.
  2. Those who returned to engage with the content later.
  3. Those who arrived at the page but never returned.

I'm going to use these groups in the next chapter to help us understand the user experience. For now, the recommendations I'm providing are aimed at the third (and largest) group. Although we attracted over 500 people to the data strategy website, less than half of these users engaged in sessions lasting longer than 2 minutes.

In general, this occurs when websites fail to make good first impressions. Some research has shown that it takes as little as 50 milliseconds for users to form opinions about the visual appeal of a website. Similarly, Amazon famously found that for every 100ms of latency they lost 1% in sales.

If we want to improve our engagement metrics, then we need to inspire confidence. Here are two recommendations:

  • Good design and user testing

    This involves conducting user testing earlier in the design process and allowing it to inform the entire project.

  • Improving page load times

    Latency causes people to lose confidence in the content. I recommend moving away from GCpedia, which added an average of 9 seconds of latency to the page. This will also help grow our audience.
